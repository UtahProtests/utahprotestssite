What is the purpose of protests?

Protesting and other resistance can take many different forms. Historically, protests have been a show of numbers and force and a threat about what would happen if our demands are not met. Some still follow these guidelines today. Currently, many protests instead are a tool for connecting, organizing, and setting up for other forms of resistance such as preparing for strikes or boycotts, supporting or denouncing candidates, or forming unions. Protests are not what create change alone, but instead a stepping stone that needs to be utilized for tasks that require more participation and more organization.

What makes an effective boycott?

It is important for individuals to stop buying as part of a boycott, but that alone is not enough for it to be effective. There needs to be targetted efforts where large groups of people are all keeping money away from the same establishment. In addition, there needs to be efforts to spread the word: online, protesting in front of the store, filling and leaving carts unattended, refusing to work there, and any other methods of getting others to join the efforts and to agitate the business. On top of this, The purpose of the boycott needs to be clearly stated and demands need to be set if the establishment wants business to resume as it used to. As an oversimplified example, Target needs to reimplement their DEI policies before we are willing to shop or work there again. The BDS Movement is a great resource for how to boycott effectively for Palestine.

Protest Safety Recommendations

Physical Safety

By far the best thing that you can do is to be generally prepared while protesting. Wear appropriate clothing. Bring water and food and hydrate and eat beforehand. Sleep enough and show up with a first aid kit. Check the weather and bring an umbrella for shade or precipitation. Be alert and looking around you at all times, especially whenever vehicles are nearby.


Another great tip is to have a group of people on bikes surrounding the group, especially if marching or if vehicles are closeby. They can direct the group and be a clear boundary between pedestrians and vehicles.

Cybersecurity Recommendations

There is no way to be fully safe or secure online, but there are lots of ways to be more secure. Take all of the steps that you can to become more secure, but there is always some level of risk involved. The most secure thing you can do is write things down physically and meet in person.

Research Generally Good Cybersecurity Practices
- If you are unfamiliar with classic cybersecurity practices it is worth the time to look them up, there are countless good resources for this out there. Tips such as how to make good passwords, not click on fishing links, not sharing personal information, etc. We will be adding a bit more to these basics with options that are popular and sometimes more useful for protesters, but would be good advice for anybody.

General Recommendations
- DO NOT SHARE PICTURES OR VIDEOS OF PROTESTORS WITHOUT THEIR CONSENT! Especially if their faces are visible. This can put you and them in danger. If you do have permission and want to share a picture, screenshot it before posting, do not post original photos. Videos you will need to look at options of how to clear the metadata of your identifying information.
- Turn off your location when not using it and block apps from having access to it if they do not need it.
- Turn off cell phone and leave it in another room while having serious conversations. Yes it is that deep.
- Whenever possible, don't bring your phone or other technology at all to protests. If needed, switch off all services not required.
- Turn off cell service and turn airplane mode on whenever you feel like being a bit more secure. In addition turn off bluetooth, which may separately need to be switched off in the settings depending on your device. The swipedown does not usually actually turn it all the way off - especially low power bluetooth.
- Physically block off cameras when you are not using them with something like a sticky note. Once again, yes it is that serious.
- Disconnect services from your personal information or name whenever possible.
- Use aliases instead of your actual name whenever possible.
- Set all of your media use to have reoccurring times to be deleted. Like search history every week or your computer's trash bin to every month, as two arbitrary examples.
- We would love to recommend not having social media, but unfortunately it seems to be the main place to learn of resistance efforts here. Until that is remedied, please be responsible with your social media use and look into good cybersecurity practices for it's use.

Use Signal
- Signal is an app for texting and messaging that is more secure, but not completely secure. If somebody has your phone they may still be able to access the information. Good practices include hiding content from message notifications, using aliases, not sharing personal information unless absolutely necessary, deleting chats and messages when you are done with them, and setting up a PIN or password for Signal.

Use Proton Mail
- Proton has email services as well as a few others like documents, spreadsheets, VPN, file drive, authenticator or password manager. Be warned that Proton does typically comply with law enforcement as required by Switzerland, where the company is based. However, it is arguably a better option than Google or Microsoft might be for security, and you have the benefit of boycotting those companies more as recommended by the BDS Movement for Palestine.

Use a Password Manager
- A password manager is like a lockbox for all of your passwords. Have you ever seen your browser save your passwords or even generate a secure one for you? That's what this is, but better than your browser doing it. Some of them even have extensions to make it as easy as what is built into your browser. We recommend Proton or Bitwarden, but there are plenty of good ones out there. Even better than this - writing down passwords physically is the most secure way. Make sure you have at least one backup spot they are written if you lose the first.

Change your Search Engine
- Search engines are so insecure and gather so much data on you. Big ones such as Google, Bing (Microsoft), or Yahoo are to be avoided. Sadly, there is no perfect search engine out there for security or privacy. We recommend DuckDuckGo or Ecosia, but they have their issues as well. Find one that works for you, and make sure to hide AI results and to opt out of any data collection they may allow you to. Once again avoid those like Google or Microsoft that are being boycotted by the BDS Movement.

Change your Browser
- Browsers and search engines are sometimes tied but they have a few differences. Like search engines, they collect lots of data about you and sell it. Stay away from Chrome or Chromium or Microsoft Edge. Safari is slightly better but there are alternatives on a computer. All iphone browsers are technically just modified versions of Safari, but they can still be a bit better too. We recommend Librewolf, or just plain Firefox while opting out of everything related to data collection or ai. It also may be looking into Brave or Opera.

Advanced Recommendation - Switch your Operating System
- Even after completing all of the above recommendations, it is unfortunately not going to save you from Mac's OSX or Microsoft's Windows from also tracking you or compromising you to some extent. To avoid this, we recommend switching to a Linux distro of some kind. It is a bit tricky, and less user friendly, but anybody could do it with enough time if they really care about their security. There are lots of resources on this so go and find them.

Activism Checklist

Don't know where to begin? First make sure you have done all of the following items.

  • Register to Vote
    • Be informed
    • ACTUALLY VOTE WHEN THE TIME COMES!
    • Register with a party so you can vote in the primary elections
    • Make sure to keep your voter registration information updated, especially your address
  • Attend a Protest
    • While there, talk to THREE new people and ONE new organization
  • Download the 5 Calls app and call EVERY DAY
    • This app cannot be recommended enough. Easiest way to reach out to your legislators.
  • Volunteer on a Consistent Schedule
    • Find an organization you like and reach out.
    • Ideally you would be participating weekly.
  • Consistently Attend your City Council Meeting
    • Consider asking questions, voicing your opinion, or even running to be a part of it.
  • Boycott Companies Investing in what you are Protesting
    • Start with the list from the BDS movement (above) and expand from there
  • Attend or Watch at least ONE Training
    • Some options are listed above, alternatively, nokings.org usually has some planned.
  • Create a Signal Group Chat with at least FIVE Neighbors
    • This takes time. Start by introducing yourself and offering to help with anything they may need.
    • Use it to get to know them, share information, and help one another. Grow community with those closest to you.
    • Grow the relationships and members of the chat over time.
  • Host a Night where you all Call and Write to your Legislators.
    • Invite just a few friends, your neighbors, people you met at protests, family members, or anybody else who is willing to come.
  • Donate to a Good Cause or Organization
    • Everybody has something to give, even if it is old clothes. Research politics, profit incentives, and affiliations beforehand.
  • Get at least ONE other Person to Complete this Whole List
